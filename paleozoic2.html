Aegrocassis

The aegrocassis was a giant, aquatic ancient filter-feeding arthropod that lived during the Paleozoic Era, specifically during the Ordovician period, roughly 480 million years ago. It was one of the largest known anomalocaridids, reaching lengths of up to 2 meters, bigger than the anomalocaris of the Cambrian Era. This creature had a torpedo-shaped head, an elongated body with 11 segments, and two sets of flaps along the sides of each segment, which were most likely used for swimming and stabilization. Unlike most anomalocaridids, which were active predators, the aegrocassis was more of a filter feeder, using modified head appendages to sieve plankton from the water, similarly to some modern whales. The aegrocassis bares a strong ressemblance to another creature called the hurdia, which lived way before the time of the aegrocassis's existence, existing during the Cambrian Period.

Meganeura

Now onto land animals, the meganeura is known for being one of the largest insects that ever lived, having lived during the Late Carboniferous period, roughly 300 million years ago. The meganeura took on the appearance of a giant dragonfly and having a wingspan of up to two feet, making it larger than any modern dragonfly. It is widely considered to have been a predatory insect that likely hunted other insects and even small amphibians. The meganeura also possessed multi-faced eyes, similar to modern dragonflies, which helped it spot prey while airborne. Many scientists have theorized that the higher oxygen levels that filled the Earth's atmosphere during the Carboniferous period may have contributed to the meganeura's large size like many other giant insects that lived during the same time period.

Dunkleosteus

The dunkleosteus was a giant prehistoric fish that lived during the Late Devoinian period, approximately 380 to 360 million years ago. This creature was a top predator during the Late Devonian period, meaning it was at the top of the foodchain during its time. Unlike most fish, the dunkleosteus didn't have true teeth, but instead, possessed sharp bony plates that sharpened as the jsaw opened and closed. This also led the dunkleosteus to having a very powerful bite force, estimated to be up to 80,000 pounds per square inch at certain points of the jaw. What made this prehistoric fish very unique was its armored exterior. The dunkleosteus had a bony, armored head and thorax, providing protection against other predators and possibly other dunkleosteus who may pose a threat. It is estimated to reach lengths of 20 to 29 feet.

Arthropleura

The arthropleura is a giant millipiede-like creature that lived during the Carboniferous Period, between 359 and 299 million years ago. They are widely known for their terrifying appearance in being the largest known arthropod to have ever lived on land. Estimates show them to reach lengths of up to 2.5 meters and weighed around 50 kilograms. Although their huge sizes mixed with their millipede appearances can give off terrified impressions, the arthropleura was primarily herbivorous. They are believed to have fed on leaf litter, other plant detritus, and potentially even larger plants, fruits, and seeds. Due to its large size, the arthropleura had some natural predators. As a way of defense, they possessed the ability to rear up, potentially to appear more intimidating, and may have also been able to spray chemicals, similarly to modern day millipedes. Just like the meganeura, the arthropleura's massive size may be due to the huge oxygen levels present in Earth's atompshere during the Carboniferous Period.
